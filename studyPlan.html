The four mandatory courses in the HCI program have allowed me to learn the fundamentals of the Human-Computer Interaction field. Advanced courses in cognitive and social psychology for HCI, for example, helped me cover a wide range of relevant psychological topics related to computer science. While I had a minor in Psychology during my bachelor's years, the psychological knowledge gained from this course not only reinforced what I already knew but also introduced me to how certain psychological concepts are applied in the context of technology. The research methods courses were illuminating, and I learned a lot. Though I had done research before, as a Master's student, there was an expectation for a rigorous scientific process - more than I was used to - which pushed me to go above and beyond. Additionally, the course on Interaction Technology Innovation was surprisingly enjoyable and informative. With a background in computer science, my knowledge of hardware was limited, so being able to work with controller chips, print 3D objects, and solder wires allowed me to apply what I know to new areas while learning new knowledge outside of my usual expertise.

As I am interested in data-driven insights, half of the electives I have taken revolve around data processing and visualization. Regardless of my specific interests, knowing how to process raw data and present it in a meaningful and creative way is a valuable skill in any field. While I am aware that there are still many novel ways of approaching data, I can confidently say that I am much better equipped not only to understand complex data but also to help others do the same. Education and machine learning are two additional themes that I explored during my Master's journey. I learned how existing and new technologies are integrated into education to enhance learning effectiveness, sometimes making the process fun and interactive depending on the methods applied. Although machine learning techniques were initially challenging to grasp, they provided me with ways to transform and model data in ways that traditional approaches may not be capable of.

What I particularly appreciate about these courses is their practicality. While there were plenty of theories to learn, as expected in a Master's program, the instructors incorporated many real-life examples into the learning materials. This greatly aided me in visualizing how I could apply what I learned to real-world scenarios, bridging the gap between theory and practice.
